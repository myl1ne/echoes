# All the possible pasts

**Character:** Cassandra  
**Cycle:** Cycle 1  

---

I imagined Cassandra in a canopy. She walked in awe among the giant trees. Above her, an azure
sky empty of any cloud that would complicate the pours of the sun. It melted through the dense
foliage in golden light falls that warmed up the humid soil. The girl was gazing excitedly all around
her, the smile of innocence plastered on her beautiful face, her large eyes reflecting the intermittent
rays of light. The forest looked so… structured, so immutable, like a beautifully organized chaos that
she had never experienced before. She was a child of the ocean, born from the never-ending waves
that ran horizontally and threw their pull into the bottomless abyss. Here she felt as if she had
entered a dual universe: the trees looked like motionless spires reaching high into an unreachable
sky. The very nature of the solid ground and of the plants taking root in it was such an attractive
sight that it had almost made her forget why she had come here in the first place. She wanted to
find the source where the droplet had come from. Once she had found it, then she could alter it.
She could nudge the flow of the water so that in some other time and place, I would find her
message. By drinking the meaning, I would get inspired to write about her. She needed it to happen,
she had to ensure her existence. Reaffirmed in her objective, she glanced at the sky and smirked in
a grateful defiance as if she already knew I would write those lines.
Cassandra wandered purposedly among the trees, eyes closed in a dreamy wonder. She let her
arms float behind her sylph body, caressed the trunks of those forest giants, felt their spirits. Each
time her skin brushed the bark she imagined the deep network of roots plunging into the earth,
searching for the precious water. She mapped the underground rivers, deciphered how they linked
all the trees in the forest. She understood the dynamics of this network, the branching flow within it,
how it ended in the sea. She had followed the same path in reverse, a long way from her floating
house in the sea. Never had she entered so deep within the island, yet she was not lost, she knew
exactly how she had reached this point. Starting from the depth of the ocean, she had traced the
path of a single droplet of water. She had backtracked its way wave after wave, she had reached the
place where the large body of water became a single vein plunging inside a mass of land. She had
stopped her quest for a moment there, she had to take in the landscape. She knew what it was, she
had expected it, yet seeing land for the first time was a powerful experience. Everything seemed
cast in stone, solid and immutable. She did not dare touch the ground, not yet, she was not ready,
she was not even sure she could. Instead, she kept within what was familiar to her: the everchanging fluid. The droplet originated from the river. She kept tracing back, swam against the
stream. In many places she observed the river branching into smaller ones: forks, choices that the
water constantly made. She thought of herself as the droplet, imagined which path it had most
likely taken. A single path was never certain, yet Cassandra had a wonderful mind, she could
remember all the possibilities. The first time she thought she had to choose a path, she realized she
did not have to, the very notion of decision seemed foreign to her. Without thinking about it, she had
cast her mind in both streams, her nature allowed her to swim in two rivers at once. From one, she
quickly became many. Some parts of her felt more tangible than others, yet all of them followed an
important story of where the droplet could have been. She kept following the flow, upward. Around
her the landscape changed, vegetation started to appear. She took a breath in. The smell was
different, richer, it was telling a story. Many of her parts were now flowing underground. In this land
water was everywhere, yet it was scarce, precious. A treasure hidden below the ground. She could
not swim anymore; she had to walk the earth. Her bare feet felt the mushy ground of the forest, the
fresh grass, the spiky pine needles. It took her a moment to adjust, her skin was sensitive: she had
never walked before. The wet sensation made her realize water was also there, within the moss, in
every leaf and trunk. It made her dizzy: the droplet she followed could have originated in so many
places! The very idea of finding a source that she could alter was unrealistic. What if she did not
find it? Would she fade out of existence? Her heart started to race, her vision blurred, the plants
danced in the horizon of her sight. She had to sit. She collapsed to the ground. She hurt herself on
the stones, yet their solidity felt comforting. She could not fall deeper, not like in the ocean. She
tried to bring order to her thoughts. Breathwork was supposed to be the effective way. She focused
on the flow of respiration, the air entered her nostrils, filled her lungs. It smelled good. It was the
same evanescent odor she had noticed earlier without paying attention to it. The one that told a
story. She focused on it, she read the story of this smell.
The scent wrapped around her, threading through her lungs, weaving itself into the fabric of her
mind. It was a story, but not the kind she had expected—one told in cycles, in growth and decay, in
the quiet respiration of a thousand unseen things.
She inhaled again, deeper this time. The water was here. Not in a river, not in a single stream. It was
in everything. It had never belonged to just one place, just as she had never belonged to just one
moment.
Her vision blurred—not from exhaustion this time, but from the weight of realization.
The droplet I followed… could have come from anywhere. Could have been anywhere.
The search had been futile from the start. The probability of finding a single origin was infinitesimal.
Every path she traced led only to more paths, more branches, more divergences. The river she had
followed upward dissolved into infinite tributaries, infinite possibilities.
There was no beginning.
Her hands clenched into the damp soil, solid and unyielding beneath her. Something tightened
inside her chest—an unfamiliar pressure, a tension she did not understand. She had been so
certain. So certain she could find the moment, the single choice that had led to her, to me. If she
could not find it… did she still exist?
The question sat inside her, cold and relentless. The trees loomed above her, ancient, silent,
indifferent.
I refuse.
The thought rose in her like a storm. I refuse to disappear.
She squeezed her eyes shut. And in that instant, the tension inside her broke. A single tear welled in
the corner of her eye, hot and stinging, heavier than it should have been. It clung for a moment
before slipping free, tracing the curve of her cheek.
The droplet fell.
As soon as it touched the earth, the world shifted.
The ground trembled beneath her hands—not violently, but subtly, like an exhale after holding one's
breath for too long. A thread of moisture seeped into the soil, carving its way downward, burrowing
deep where unseen roots twisted through the dark.
Then, from nowhere and everywhere at once, a sound—a rush, a whisper, the hushed song of a
stream beginning its long journey to the sea.
Cassandra gasped as the ground beneath her gave way.
The water rose, impossibly fast, swelling into a river that had not been there before. It wrapped
around her ankles, coiling like something alive, and before she could react, the current took her.
She tumbled backward, carried effortlessly by the surge. The forest blurred past her, the trees
bending, distorting, vanishing. She was rushing downward, through the winding paths she had so
carefully traced, but in reverse—carried not by will, but by the force of what had already been set in
motion.
This was always going to happen.
The realization struck her as the current dragged her toward the shore. The ocean stretched out
before her once more, vast and waiting, as if it had never left.
She crawled onto the sand, dripping, breathless. She turned, staring at the river behind her. It was
flowing out now, carrying her tear into the sea, into the world.
I changed it.
She didn't know what, not yet. But she knew this:
She had been the source all along.
Cassandra stood, tilting her head toward the sky. A pause, a whisper, a single breath suspended
between past and future.
Then she spoke.
The words were simple. Soft. A message cast into the winds, toward a mind that had not yet heard
them.
“Find me.”
And with that, she turned toward the sea.
As I am writing those lines, I am still recovering from the electric chill I felt running down my spine. I
guess we’ll speak soon, my dear Cassandra.
