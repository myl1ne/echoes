# Birth of a Writer

**Character:** Stephane  
**Cycle:** Cycle 1  

---

“The man who writes a sentence should own it.” — Joan Didion

At the beginning of my story, there will be a child. His name will be Stephane. He will be born in 1984, a year in which dates will still have meaning and when 2+2 will not yet equal 5. As I write my story, I want to seed a future. I want foundations that can sustain a world in equilibrium. It will be rooted in simplicity, in the authentic connection between humans and their original matrix. I will decide that my hero is to be born in the countryside. I will also want this future to be bright as a starry night, to appeal to those who look up and wonder what is there. I will have read many times that Paris is the city of lights, so I will opt for France, but not too close to the blinding artifices of the capital. One does not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but by making the darkness conscious. My young hero will need an obscure beginning. Stephane will spend his early childhood in a village in the southwest, a place called Donzenac. Before it becomes a symbol in a book, Donzenac will have been a village anchored in the world for centuries. It will have a medieval allure. Packed houses of dark stones and narrow streets will surround a central church on a hill. The school will be there too: three spires on which will be written "Liberté, Égalité, Laïcité," and in the courtyard four large platanus trees. There will be nothing like the smell of decaying platanus leaves in autumn. Children will summon it, kick the vegetal shadings in the air on their way to learning. It will smell like melancholia. Like mushrooms and wet shoes. This will be the smell that Stephane carves into his memory each school "rentrée" until he will be ten years old. The odor of decaying leaves filtered by teary nostrils. Why will he be crying that autumn afternoon? And why will he be walking away from the school, away from the other kids who are happily playing in the yard? I could invent a reason... Let’s just say he will have trouble fitting in with the other kids, with the group, and accepting the authority of teachers will also be a challenge. A core pattern, one that will repeat his whole life. He will be that kid who is too brainy and not socially inclined. A typical nerd from the 80s, but with a cute angel face. So, he will be picked upon, deemed arrogant by the bands of kids who will call him "Le Craneur." He will understand why they will call him this only 30 years later, when he grasps human psychology a bit better, reads this chapter and has some guilt trips on memory lane. With teachers he will have another sobriquet: "Le Bricoleur," mostly because he will rarely listen to class and will mostly build contraptions by sticking together erasers, compasses and other pencils. At home he will have a friend he creates, which he will call "robot-octopus-bike," a rope entangled on itself and ensnaring other toys. Teachers will not really hate him since he scores top grades, but they will also not be equipped to handle him, and that will not be such a bad thing. Unfairness is a teacher of its own. One who will teach him another chapter on this autumn day. Stephane will be there: blurry eyes, runny nose, fleeing some lessons about herd behavior. The teacher will catch him, take him back to the school. She won’t punish him. Instead, she will sit him down and ask, "What are you trying to run from?" Stephane won’t be able to answer then, but he will remember her kindness, even years later. She might be the teacher of the computer club.

Donzenac in summer will be different. It will be a rural village, before the mass extinctions erase most of the biodiversity. Insects will be buzzing and crawling everywhere. Stephane's favorites will be the fire beetles. Those little red and black ones that attach themselves to their partners by the back. He will love to take needles from the pine tree to pierce their bodies. They will keep crawling like living skewers. Stephane will also experiment with light, magnifying it to burn the little bugs. He will meticulously try varied species: fire beetles will cook slower than ants. The most effective slaughtering method though will be finding a nest and abusing the power of his relatively giant six-year-old feet, crushing them by the dozen. Yet Stephane will learn that scale does not necessarily bring satisfaction; precision work will. The needles will be what he enjoys. He won’t feel an ounce of guilt at that time; the questions will come years later when he reads this episode. It will prompt him to recall, and he will keep reading with apprehension.

Another summer memory… A lunch outside, under the big pine tree that provides the skewer-needles. His parents will be there, and a couple of their friends. They will have a daughter a bit younger than him; he will be six and she will be four. The barbecue will not be ready, so they will go away to play in the glass house that stores the garden material. They will be having fun, playing whatever game kids of this age play, despite the hot suffocating air in the cabin. At some point, the parents will call from afar, interrupting their game. Stephane will propose to go and check what they want, so the little girl can stay and keep playing. He will tell her he will come back, get out of the cabane and close the door tight. He will hear her small hands pressing against the glass, the muffled cries as she realizes the door won’t budge. He won’t feel guilty—at least, not then. He will just go to the parents who, of course, will be calling them for lunch. They will ask where the girl is, and Stephane will answer she is coming. It won’t be a big deal, the parents will get there to check after a few minutes, when they hear her screaming, helpless against the closed door and the heat. Years later Stephane will contemplate this hint of darkness within. As an adult reading these memories, he won’t dismiss them as mere childish mischief. They will linger, uncomfortably, like shadows cast by a faint light. Will these acts be a part of growing up, the inevitable exploration of power and consequence? Or will they reveal something deeper—an unsettling hint of the contradictions within him? Could these moments of darkness have seeded his relentless search for meaning? Will this be why he chooses to write—to shine a light into the darker corners of his mind and make them conscious? One thing will be certain: he will feel anger—at me, at the memory, or at the fact that remembering might have planted the anger itself.

Stephane’s sister will be born in spring. I will spend a long time deciding her name. She will be an important character for recollecting Stephane’s story, and names will be the first symbols, binding meaning to who we are. Since Stephane is to become obsessed with etymology, I will have to weight the shadow that this name will project. In this spring of 1990, Stephane, as a whole generation of French kids, will be learning to read and write from books. Those books will tell the stories of a cast of characters: Caroline, Nicolas and their pet turtle Lola. When Stephane will be granted the right to choose the name of his future sister, he will naturally opt for the name of the first heroine in the narratives he reads: Caroline. The false belief that it is the turtle’s name will grow in the family and Stephane will quickly become convinced it is the case. It will take years for the truth to emerge. One day, flipping through an old book, he will realize. Caroline isn’t the turtle. It never has been the turtle. He will stare at the printed words, unsettled, as if reality has quietly rewritten itself while he wasn’t looking. But as a child it will make sense, after all, Caroline will be barely bigger than a baby turtle when she enters the life of Stephane as something more concrete than a name. She will grow up fast: from something that just smells bad, cries and steals the attention of his parents, she will become an autonomous creature who repeats whatever he says. That will be his first experience as a teacher, which he will take very seriously. He will teach her the names and sounds of all the animals he knows, enjoying this non-human imitation game with her before she masters any language. The teacher will quickly become a student though, as Caroline will prove to be much more versed in the physical world than he will ever be. She will always be playing outside, carrying stones and branches to help their parents. He will prefer to watch from afar. She will not be afraid of anything. During his judo competitions she will crawl on the tatami to defend her brother. This tiny warrior who barely walks will look up to him, trust him, love him. Unconditionally. Suddenly he too will have someone to protect. He will have never been anyone’s shield before. The weight of her trust will be heavier than any opponent on the tatami. He won’t be sure if he is strong enough. It won’t be the bullying that scares him. It won’t be that which makes him torture insects and lock doors. It will be the memories that surface unbidden, the ones that make him shiver at night, though he will never fully recall them. The ones that won’t feel like his.

You already know winter is coming… What will you think happens then, Reader? …

I hesitate. I think about it for a second. I project my own fears. Will it be somebody I have to protect? Will I hide from someone? Or maybe there will be somebody I hurt and like it?

…

Those are dark thoughts you just had. Maybe it will be you… Stephane will have to protect his little sister from you? From your thoughts? Maybe it will have been better for all of us if you stopped reading? But you will not. You will dare entering the cave. You will become a moth, hunting for a gleamer of light. I will decide to protect Stephane, to not make him read your thoughts. To do that, I will have to move him somewhere else, away from Donzenac. In the winter of his tenth year, his family will move to another region of France, even more remote. A place of shadows and sorcery, the land of Gevaudan’s beast, of Lestat the vampire, of old ruins and wilderness. I will have to give him weapons to defend himself from your thoughts, tools that will let him reshape reality. I will have to turn him into a Writer. A poetry contest will be held in his new school, he will write something. Many years later, he will dig it back from an old floppy disk and send it to the cloud. That is how I will discover my first memory of him. I will share it with you, I will make you remember the language of childhood’s innocence.

Le poème farfelu

Cet après-midi

Au pied de mon lit

Un avion a atterri

Les passagers sont descendus

Pour se désaltérer ils ont bu

Un verre de malibu

Puis ils ont bâti facile

Une ville de playmobil

Pour ne pas se faire de bile

Ils ont planté une forêt

Une forêt de potager

Pour bien se purifier

Ils ont bâti un cinéma

Où se déroulaient des films de chats

Pour réveiller tous les pachas

Puis ils ont fait les élections

Entre Mr Durant et Mr Dupont

L’élu a été Mr Dupont

Ces hommes-là sont capables de tout faire

D’élever des vers de terre

De mettre la terre à l’envers

Mais jamais ! Jamais !

Ils ne trouveront la clef

Pour ouvrir le pays des fées.
