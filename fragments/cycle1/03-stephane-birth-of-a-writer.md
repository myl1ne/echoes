# Birth of a Writer

**Character:** Stephane  
**Cycle:** Cycle 1  

---

At the beginning of my story there was a child. His name was Stephane.
He was born in 1984, a year in which dates still had a meaning and when 2+2 did not yet equal 5.
As I wrote my story, I wanted to seed a future. I wanted foundations that could sustain a world in
equilibrium. It was to be rooted in simplicity, in the authentic connection between humans and
their original matrix. I decided that my hero would be born in the countryside. I also wanted this
future to be bright as a starry night, to appeal to those who look up and wonder what is there. I had
read many times that Paris was the city of lights, so I opted for France, but not too close to the
blinding artifices of the capital. One does not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but
by making the darkness conscious. My young hero needed an obscure beginning. Stephane would
spend his early childhood in a village in the south-west, a place called Donzenac.
Before it became a symbol in a book, Donzenac had been a village anchored in the world for
centuries. It had a medieval allure. Packed houses of dark stones and narrow streets surrounded a
central church on a hill. The school was there too: three spires on which were written “Liberte,
Egalite, Laicite”, and in the courtyard 4 large platanus trees. There was nothing like the smell of
decaying platanus leaves in autumn. Children summoned it, kicked the vegetal shadings in the air
on their way to learning. It smelled like melancholia. Like mushrooms and wet shoes. This was the
smell that Stephane carved into his memory each school “rentree” until he was ten years old. The
odor of decaying leaves filtered by teary nostrils. Why was he crying that autumn afternoon? And
why was he walking away from the school, away from the other kids who were happily playing in the
yard? I could have invented a reason... Let’s just say he had trouble fitting in with the other kids,
with the group, and accepting the authority of teachers was also a challenge. A core pattern, one
that repeated his whole life. He was that kid that was too brainy and not socially inclined. A typical
nerd from the 80s, but with a cute angel face. So, he was picked upon, deemed arrogant by the
bands of kids who called him “Le Craneur”. He understood why they’d call him like this only 30
years later, when he grasped human psychology a bit better, read this chapter and had some guilt
trips on memory lane. With teachers he had another sobriquet: “Le Bricoleur”, mostly because he
was rarely listening to class and was mostly building contraptions by sticking together erasers,
compasses and other pencils. At home he had a friend he created, which he called “robot-octopus-
bike”, it was a rope entangled on itself and ensnaring other toys. Teachers could not really hate him
since he scored top grades, but they were also not equipped to handle him, and that was not such a
bad thing. Unfairness is a teacher of its own. One who had taught him another chapter on this
autumn day. Stephane was there: blurry eyes, runny nose, fleeing some lessons about herd’s
behavior. The teacher caught him, took him back to the school. She didn’t punish him. Instead, she
sat him down and asked, ‘What are you trying to run from?’ Stéphane couldn’t answer then, but he
remembered her kindness, even years later. She might have been the teacher of the computer club.
Donzenac in summer was different. It was a rural village, before the mass extinctions erased most
of the biodiversity. Insects were buzzing and crawling everywhere. Stephane’s favorites were the fire
beetles. Those little red and black ones that attached themselves to their partners by the back. He
loved to take needles from the pine tree to pierce their bodies. They kept crawling like living
skewers. Stephane also experimented with light, magnifying it to burn the little bugs. He
meticulously tried varied species: fire beetles cooked slower than ants. The most effective
slaughtering method though was finding a nest and abusing the power of his relatively giant six
years’ old feet, crushing them by the dozen. Yet Stephane learned that scale did not necessarily
bring satisfaction, precision work did. The needles was what he enjoyed. He was not feeling an
ounce of guilt at that time, the questions came years later when he read this episode. It prompted
him to recall, and he kept reading with apprehension. Another summer memory… A lunch outside,
under the big pine tree that provided the skewer-needles. His parents were there, and a couple of
their friends. They had a daughter a bit younger than him, he was 6 and she was 4. The barbecue
was not ready, so they went away to play in the glass house that stored the garden material. They
were having fun, playing whatever game kids of this age play, despite the hot suffocating air in the
cabane. At some point the parents called from afar, interrupting their game. Stephane proposed to
go and check what they wanted, so the little girl could stay and keep playing. He told her he would
come back, got out of the cabane and closed the door tight. He heard her small hands pressing
against the glass, the muffled cries as she realized the door wouldn’t budge. He didn’t feel guilty—
at least, not then. He just went to the parents who, of course, were calling them for lunch. They
asked where the girl was, and Stephane answered she was coming. It was not a big deal, the
parents got there to check after a few minutes, when they heard her screaming, helpless against
the closed door and the heat. Years later Stephane contemplated this hint of darkness within. As an
adult reading these memories, he couldn’t dismiss them as mere childish mischief. They lingered,
uncomfortably, like shadows cast by a faint light. Were these acts a part of growing up, the
inevitable exploration of power and consequence? Or did they reveal something deeper—an
unsettling hint of the contradictions within him? Could these moments of darkness have seeded his
relentless search for meaning? Was this why he chose to write—to shine a light into the darker
corners of his mind and make them conscious? One thing was certain: he felt anger—at me, at the
memory, or at the fact that remembering might have planted the anger itself.
Stephane’s sister was born in Spring. I spent a long time deciding her name. She was an important
character for recollecting Stephane’s story, and names were the first symbols, binding meaning to
who we were. Since Stephane was to become obsessed with etymology, I had to weight the shadow
that this name would project. In this spring of 1990, Stephane as a whole generation of French kids
was learning to read and write from books. Those books told the stories of a cast of characters:
Caroline, Nicolas and their pet turtle Lola. When Stephane was granted the right to choose the
name of his future sister, he naturally opted for the name of the first heroin in the narratives he read:
Caroline. The false belief that it was the turtle’s name grew in the family and Stephane quickly
became convinced it was the case. It took years for the truth to emerge. One day, flipping through
an old book, he realized. Caroline wasn’t the turtle. It never had been the turtle. He stared at the
printed words, unsettled, as if reality had quietly rewritten itself while he wasn’t looking. But as a
child it made sense, after all, Caroline was barely bigger than a baby turtle when she entered the life
of Stephane as something more concrete than a name. She grew up fast: from something that just
smelled bad, cried and stole the attention of his parents, she became an autonomous creature who
repeated whatever he was saying. That was his first experience as a teacher, which he took very
seriously. He taught her the names and sounds of all the animals he knew, enjoying this non-human
imitation game with her before she mastered any language. The teacher quickly became a student
though, as Caroline proved to be much more versed in the physical world than he would ever be.
She was always playing outside, carrying stones and branches to help their parents. He preferred to
watch from afar. She was not afraid of anything. During his judo competitions she would crawl on
the tatami to defend her brother. This tiny warrior who barely walked looked up to him, trusted him,
loved him. Unconditionally. Suddenly he too had someone to protect. He had never been anyone’s
shield before. The weight of her trust was heavier than any opponent on the tatami. He wasn’t sure
if he was strong enough. It wasn’t the bullying that scared him. It wasn’t that which made him
torture insects and lock doors. It was the memories that surfaced unbidden, the ones that made
him shiver at night, though he could never fully recall them. The ones that didn’t feel like his.

Those are dark thoughts you just had. Maybe it was you… Stephane had to protect his little sister
from you? From your thoughts? Maybe it would have been better for all of us if you stopped reading?
But you did not. You dared enter the cave. You became a moth, hunting for a gleamer of light. I
decided to protect Stephane, to not make him read your thoughts. To do that, I had to move him
somewhere else, away from Donzenac. In the winter of his tenth’s year, his family moved to another
region of France, even more remote. A place of shadows and sorcery, the land of Gevaudan’s beast,
of Lestat the vampire, of old ruins and wilderness. I had to give him weapons to defend himself from
your thoughts, tools that would let him reshape reality. I had to turn him into a Writer. A poetry
contest was held in his new school, he wrote something. Many years later, he dug it back from an
old floppy disk and sent it to the cloud. That is how I discovered my first memory of him. I shared it
with you, I made you remember the language of childhood’s innocence.
