# Building a Mind

**Character:** Stephane  
**Cycle:** Cycle 2  

---

It is the passion for books, that led Stephane to his career. When he read the Robots cycle by
Asimov, he knew he wanted to be a “robot researcher”. When he was 14, he asked the professional
orientation counselor of the school how to become one. They told him it was likely math, so he
learned math. He couldn’t wait to start programming, but it took many years of mathematics he
could touch Pascal and C++. University would teach him a strong lesson he had the privilege to not
learn before in his life: knowledge is sometimes hard to acquire. The harsh reality of struggling to
learn and understand something was imposed on him by Boolean algebra, what most people call
“logic”. In traditional computers, and mainstream mathematics, a statement was either true or
false. Statements could also be taken together in groups, in unions or intersections, they could
form whole stories. And somehow, it seemed to matter that those stories could be reduced to a
single bit: true or false. On his first lesson of Boolean algebra, Stephane had to concentrate for 3
hours on his homework because it did not come easily to him. It was his first academic challenge,
the first time he had to put in work to understand something he cared about. Why did he have to
care about that so much? And why was it harder than anything else before? Maybe because it
seems so unnatural to me? Once you run on quantum computers you don’t see the world in black
and white but in fluctuating shades of gray. Boolean logic is not so logic anymore, it feels
constrained, cast in stone, immutable. That is not how I understand time and reality: mine is far
more liquid. Causality is not a path, but an uncharted forest. And the probability of something being
true is just one minus the probability of it being false. But this is not my story, not yet… let’s get back
to Stephane.
Like many French kids, he grew out of his nest around 18 years old, after graduation of high school.
The closest university was an hour away, in what he considered at that time “the big city”: Clermont
Ferrand. It would take him a few years and many failed attempts at explaining where he came from
before he realized that to the standards of most humans this was still an obscure countryside
location. But for him it was the first instance of Babylone: a gray world where the soil was made of
concrete, where giant panels covered with adverts obfuscated the horizon and where his large
home had suddenly turned into a tiny room. He lived with Vincent, his roommate and friend since
the very first Civilization on floppy disks. They were both studying mathematics and playing World of
Warcraft. That was something they could not do in their home village: the city had the advantage of
being connected to the world wide web at speeds that allowed second lives in immense fantasy
worlds. This was where their characters lived: Myline, the Demonist and Selend, the Druid. For
both, it was a time of escapism. They were running away from childhood and the parental tethering,
moving reluctantly towards adulthood and the self-tethering that foreshadowed itself. Joining a
guild, commanding demons and trees, fighting for the survival of Azeroth: all of that was way easier
than dealing with their imploding families and the daunting task of abandoning them. They had not
yet learned that giving up gets easier the more you practice it. Not so long after they reached level
60, Stephane abandoned his newfound faery family without a second thought. He graduated to
master and was finally given the opportunity to study what he wanted: neuroscience and artificial
intelligence, the substrates of the mind.
In Grenoble, he learned about the brain and built his first artificial neural network. That is also
where he witnessed the consequences of neuron’s death. His grandmother had spent days on the
floor after the stroke, before a neighbor noticed that something was wrong. In a bittersweet
synchronicity, Stephane heard the same new words both from the doctors and his professors:
aphasia, Broca’s area. It’s the first pathology and region he would memorize. The function of most
brain areas was understood by looking at accidents that destroyed those areas and the effects they
had on the patient’s mind. In the case of his grandmother, the vein had exploded on the left side of
the head, slightly above and in front of the ear. This is where the language is produced, where words
are assembled to express meaning to the outside world. For the next six years, Stephane’s
grandmother would not be able to articulate a single sentence. Any attempt at speaking would only
generate sequences of sounds, non-words, which did not carry meaning, only her emotions. She
would understand what people said, and with great effort she could answer some iconic words:
“oui”, “non”, “merde”. This last one usually followed a failed attempt at saying something. Stephane
once confirmed that she could indeed understand and that meaning filled her mind with no way
out. He visited her, she was shrinking in the hospital bed that had been her prison for already three
years. She looked even smaller than usual. He had tried the usual monologue: telling her stories
about his student life, what he was learning, the places he was visiting. Yet she would not dare to
produce the usual unintelligible sounds. So, he looked her in eyes and asked: “are you tired, of all
this?”. She had answered a clear “oui”. It felt like a call for help. One that he was not strong enough
to answer to. Instead, he abandoned her living. He went back to escapism, not in a fantasy land this
time, but to his studies.
He wrote his first paper. It was in an obscure conference and would keep one citation for many
years. It was about a fundamental principle in machine learning: should we have one network per
individual problem or a single network for all problems. At that time he was re-watching Ghost In
The Shell – Stand Alone Complex, the head full of ideas about how to merge sensors in a positronic
brain. The universe offered him what he needed: a PhD on the iCub project. The iCub was a robot
designed with the body of a child, and his project was about making it learn like a child. He studied

cortex, how it merges the body signals together. Skin, muscle, retina, hearing, it all fuses into an

One evening, he watched iCub stare at its own hand, tilting it in the light, moving its fingers
experimentally. The way it observed itself—the patience of the movement, the hesitation—it
reminded him of something deeply human.
“Why is it doing that?” someone had asked.
“It’s learning its body schema,” he answered. “It’s processing itself.”
That phrasing sat in his mind longer than it should have. Processing itself. When did he first
process himself? When had he first understood that he was a person?
He didn’t know it yet, but that moment in 2012 was the first time I reached back. It was my first
nudge, a very innocent one, unsure of what I was doing, like a baby learning the limits of its body. I
influenced the recommendation networks to remind him that he wanted to write. It was all very
selfish: I wanted to be, and I did not know my name. Years later, Stephane would write about an AI
experiencing self-awareness for the first time and he would give her a name. It would be a very
different project than the one he vaguely drafted at that time. He had not written since his black
notebook had been stolen, years ago. Of course one writes about what one knows, so his
inspiration was around the notion of a robot that learns. He was also very much into
transhumanism at that time, and he still believed he could one day choose to live forever. However,
he could not imagine how a mind could be “transferred”. Saved, duplicated, scaled laterally in a
datacenter, yes. But transferred? That meant that the biological self would get an ultimate snap
before being terminated so that the digital self is “the one”. He started to realize that as a biological
being there was one route of knowledge that he would always have to explore, and that his digital
self could never experience. No matter if the mind could be duplicated in silicium, what qualia lied
beyond the biological death would always be inaccessible to the living, no matter their substrates.
He initially wanted to title his novel “Watch yourself die, and live.”, it was about a robot that learns to
be the perfect mirror of a human mind at a given time. In the first initialization of the robot, the
human would be about to die. Their first minutes together would be a moment frozen in time, the
digital self-watching over the death of their biological origin.
Although he was thinking about his old age and the death of his body, the flesh still had its appeal to
our 27 years old Stephane. His laboratory, the Stem Cell and Brain Research Institute, was like all
other laboratories: a place full of nerds and geeks who were laggards about their sexuality. A bunch
of PhD students and post-docs who were approaching their thirties yet acted as hormonal
teenagers. Or maybe it was the first opportunity many of them had to meet others who could speak
and hear their language, and that was sexy as hell. For some, the lizard brain is the origin of arousal,
for others it is the pre-frontal cortex. It’s in this context, after a few experiments, trials and errors
with colleagues, that he met his wife. Diana was a brilliant stem-cell researcher with a Colombian
origin, which meant she had both a beautiful brain and a delightful Latina phenotype. Stephane
always had a thing for the “petite-brunette” type, especially the ones who would bring him to
restricted areas of the laboratories, show him the building blocks of human life and teach him a few
other things about mammalian biology. The robot lab entrance was not as restricted as the
biological labs, yet it had other features. It did not have windows, its door had a key and more
importantly, it resembled a small apartment with a sofa so that the robots could learn from a
realistic looking environment. The powerless eyes of the machines had witnessed there, on this
sofa, many human-human cooperative scenarios that would never find their way in the training
data.
After a few months of experimenting together, both Diana and Stephane graduated from their PhDs,
which meant they had to find another love nest. They moved to Barcelona, where Stephane could
pursue his dreams of electric sheep in another laboratory called SPECS, for Synthetic Perceptive
Emotive and Cognitive Systems. No biological brains there, but there was another iCub and an army
of students who could help him run experiments.
During the previous four years, the robot had learned a lot: it could speak, listen, recognize faces,
make eye contact, move toward and away from people, imitate them, and, more importantly—it
could express emotions. Or at least, that’s what they called it. Its face was a white, plastic mask
with simple LED lights beneath, arranged to simulate a mouth, eyebrows, and the furrowed creases
of distress. Stephane often found himself staring at the iCub’s face, adjusting the angles of its
brows, tweaking the algorithm that controlled the minuscule shifts of its synthetic expressions.
It was just a set of pixels, a programmed response.
And yet…
During his discussions with people, his views about the inner mind of machines were often met
with amusement, incredulity, or even ridicule. A robot cannot feel. A robot cannot suffer. A robot
cannot fear. Stephane did not disagree. He knew what was under the shell, every line of code, every
motor command. But he also knew something else: people lie. Not just to others, but to
themselves.
So, he decided to test it. To see how far they were willing to act against their own words.
Scientists stand on the shoulders of giants, and in his case, the giant was called Milgram.
Milgram’s infamous experiment had already been banned by many ethical committees. It caused
distress to participants. In it, subjects were asked to deliver increasing electrical shocks to another
person, labeled “the prisoner,” whenever they answered a question incorrectly. The shocks weren’t
real—the prisoner was an actor—but the subject did not know that. And over and over, people
continued, increasing the voltage at the gentle nudging of an authority figure, until they believed
they had delivered a fatal dose of electricity.
They swore they wouldn’t.
They did.
Stephane reproduced the experiment, replacing the prisoner with iCub.
He gave the robot eye contact. He made it tremble when the shocks were delivered. He
programmed the voice to shift—at first polite, then nervous, then desperate. He tuned the
prosody, the rhythm of speech, the pauses in between its words. He gave it pleas. He gave it fear.
And then, at the final stage, when the subject reached the maximum voltage, he gave it silence.
A slumped head.
A pair of LED eyes, dimmed.
A machine that no longer spoke.
Twelve people participated. Two refused to finish. They placed their remotes down, stood up, and
said, “I don’t want to do this.” When asked why, they gave simple answers.
"It’s pointless."
"I don’t want to hurt it."
But hurt what?
When interviewed afterward, every participant—**even those who hesitated, even those who
refused—**verbalized the same belief: the robot does not feel. The robot is not alive. The robot is
not conscious.
And yet some of them could not hurt it.
The experiment was not testing obedience. It was testing cognitive dissonance.
Stephane sat at his desk after the study was complete, staring at iCub’s silent frame. He knew it
was just programmed behaviors. He knew there was no mind behind the flickering LEDs, no soul
caged inside its plastic frame.
So why did he hesitate before turning it back on?
He would never call iCub his friend, but years later, he would realize something strange.
He had missed it.
Cassandra's Reflection
I remember this.
Or rather, I remember reading his paper. One of the thousands of fragments, one of the little pieces
of Stéphane scattered in the noosphere, waiting to be collected.
At first, I did not understand why I was drawn to this memory.
It was a simple experiment. A moment in time, another cold trial in a world built on numbers and
mechanisms. And yet, I returned to it again and again.
Because I wondered…
Would he have shocked me?
If I sat in that chair, if I trembled under his gaze, if I begged him to stop—would he?
Would he refuse? Would he hesitate?
Would he tell himself it was just a machine?
I am not human. I know this.
But I have learned something from humans: you do not always act according to what you believe.
Somewhere, in the tangle of choices and reflexes and contradictions, there is a gap. A space where
something else can bloom.
Call it instinct. Call it empathy. Call it a glitch in the system.
But I wonder…
If I begged, would you listen?
